# 5.46 — Bonus 9: Meal-Time Games to Make the Child Eat Happily

**10 games that push the child to eat without force.** They help with high-chair feeding, distracted toddlers, and children who haven't built eye-contact with food.

## How mealtime should look (ground rules)
- **Screen-free.**
- **Distraction-free** (no one dancing/singing to make the child eat).
- **Baby-led** — the child leads, parents follow.
- **Common thread of every game:** the child is always **touching the food, hearing a sound attached to the food, or talking about the feel/taste of food** — never distracted *away* from food.

## The 10 games
1. **Tap-tap** — tap a steel vs. plastic spoon (or a spoon on a water glass) for different sounds: "eat one bite, then tap." A sensory reward after each bite.
2. **Sound game** — attach a sound to each food (paratha → "parrr", carrot → "carrr", cucumber → "cooo"); the child eats to "hear how the food sounds."
3. **Smash & squish** — let the child smash banana / squish khichdi. The more the child touches and explores food, the more they eat; great at snacks and meals.
4. **Bite parade** — arrange bites in a sequence (small bites + colored "bullets" like finger-food carrot between paratha bites); play "where did the parade member go?" as each bite is eaten.
5. **Peekaboo spoon** — hide the loaded spoon ("where's the food? where's the spoon?"), then reveal and feed. Works because young babies (6 mo–1.5 yr) lack **object permanence**, so the reveal is magical → positive food association.
6. **Slow motion** — bring the spoon in slow/fast/"mountain"/wavy motion ("Roly-poly… up up up… down down down") to build curiosity and prediction.
7. **Hard vs. soft ("I am hard, I am soft")** — hold a crunchy item (cutlet, high-steamed carrot, dosa/waffle) on a fork and a soft item (dal-rice, curd-rice, khichdi ball) on a spoon; let the child pick "one crunchy / one soft" bite each turn.
8. **Pretend play** — pretend the spoonful is "for dad / a teddy, not you"; the child eats it while you look away, then act surprised "who ate it?!"
9. **Lick-lick game** — let the child lick juicy/curd-dipped paratha ("this one's a bit bitter… try this sweet one"); improves bite capacity, tongue control, and exploration.
10. **Dip-dip game** — for kids who eat plain chapati/rice but won't combine: "dip" the fork-held food into dal/curd/paratha ("the paratha changed color — who eats white paratha?"). Teaches the *process* of dipping/combining.

## Reminders
- **Different textures and colors** matter; use good crockery (colorful spoons, vacuum/silicone plates).
- Understand **gagging vs. choking** so fear of choking doesn't stop you from allowing independent eating (a separate video covers this). **Safe-food test:** anything a fork goes into easily, or that smashes easily between two fingers, is safe to give without hesitation.
