# 1.1 — Why This Course / What Nutrients My Child Needs

Before learning *what* weaning or BLW is, parents must understand **which nutrients a child actually needs**. The essential nutrients:

1. **Calories** — needed by children (and adults) for every routine function: brain, body, development, growth. Children always need calories on a good, adequate basis.
2. **Protein** — the "building blocks." Sources: milk and milk products, non-veg, dry fruits, whole grains, lentils, paneer, curd, cheese. *How* and *in what form* to give changes age-wise, but giving protein is essential.
3. **Iron** — requirement is always high. In the first 6 months it comes from mother's milk or formula; once solids start, iron must be supplied through food in adequate quantity and proportion.
4. **Calcium** — critical because the child is growing in height and the bones are bearing increasing weight. The first milestone (neck/head control) depends on bones developing enough weight-bearing capacity. Ensure good calcium in the diet.
5. **Vitamin D** — comes from sunlight, but few parents can give the right amount of sun at the right time, so it must also come through food.
6. **Brain foods** — the child's brain and grasping are active 24 hours; they constantly observe, listen and process. Brain-supporting food should be given through the diet.
7. **Immunity boosters & probiotics** — important. Note: per doctors, a child *should* fall ill in the first year so antibodies form and they fall ill less in future. The aim isn't to prevent all illness but to reduce how long/severely the child stays ill. Probiotics and immunity boosters play a key role.
8. **Weight gainers** (not "essential" but important for parents' peace of mind) — addresses the worry that the child looks thin after 6 months.

## On supplements vs. natural sources
Parents often ask: if we already give calcium medicine, vitamin D drops, iron drops, why bother with food? Answer: doctors prescribe supplements so the child has **no deficiency**, but natural food sources will not cause excess, and the eating habits we build now become the child's future habits — so work on getting nutrients from food.

## Expected weight pattern (rule of thumb)
- Birth weight should roughly **double by ~5 months**.
- Weight should **double again by the first birthday**.
- In the second year, only ~**1–2 kg** is gained.
- After that, weight increases slowly — on average **~500 g to 1 kg**.
- A child doubling weight every 5 months continuously would be a problem; that should not keep happening.

**Goal of this session:** planned, peaceful, confident parents who know what they give is right for the child.
