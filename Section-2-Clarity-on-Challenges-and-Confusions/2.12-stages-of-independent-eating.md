# 2.12 — How to Make a Happy Independent Eater: The Stages

A child will **not** eat with their own hands from day one. There are **stages** of independent eating; a child may pass through all of them over days, or skip some. Know them so you can celebrate progress instead of worrying.

## The 6 stages
1. **Observe** — the child just stares at the food, doesn't eat. (Keeping the child hungry is not the goal — meanwhile you can offer by spoon or a preloaded spoon.)
2. **Touch** — the child touches and plays with food, nothing more. (Parents often complain "he only plays and throws" — normal, even at 8–9 months.)
3. **Smell** — the child brings food near the mouth, smells it, puts it down again. A good sign — the child is trying to understand it.
4. **Throwing** — the child throws food around. An important stage. To avoid "waste," **offer small quantities** and use **cueing**: place **one piece** on the tray, then the next, to hold the child's attention. This builds the independent-eating habit.
5. **Tasting** — the child puts food in the mouth, takes it out, puts it back, etc. The child is tasting, not yet eating.
6. **Eating** — the final stage.

## How to get the child through the stages
Seat the child in **one place, without distraction, with discipline, happily, at the right hungry timing** and feed them — the child will progress through the stages and finally become an independent eater.

## How long does it take? → Patience is everything
There's no fixed time — **5 days, 15 days, or 2 months** depending on the child. The single most important requirement is **patience**; everything else (equipment, specific high chair/plate) is secondary. Some children's lunch and dinner set as early as **7.5 months**, with dinner introduced to an 8-month-old — completely normal, no extra effort needed.

**Celebrate every step forward** — each new stage is genuine progress.
